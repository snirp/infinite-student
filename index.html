The infinite student

This page is central to my quest to learn (Python) programming. It reflects my conviction that the best way to learn is by constantly and endlessly challenging myself.

It has been said that it takes 10,000 hours of practice to truely master any skill. That is very unsatisfactory to me. First because I think that mastery comes more with the quality of the practice than the duration of the pactice. Secondly there is no reason to believe that a 'magic' number like 10,000 hours exists. Are you 'done' after that? Have you run out of challenges? Now that is a depressing thought.

So here is what I propose and will execute to teach myself programming:

  1. Formulate challenges that are tough and fun;
  2. Use the best resources;
  3. Explain, publish and discuss everything;
  4. Keep regularly spending time on it: I am never done.

This website

This website itself is the first of the challenges. I needed something that supports my mission, keeps it organized but does not get in the way.

On the one hand there is a list of challenges. This list is ever-growing and most of them should look impossible to me right now. Perfect. Any challenge should have goals ("Build a search engine") and resources ("Anatomy of a Web Search Engine, by L. Page and S. Brin"). Learn from the best :). I might not get around to getting all challenges done. Hopefully because I will learn so quickly that some seem "easy" and not worthwhile. Yep, let's hope.

On the other hand, we have the projects themselves: challenges that are (partly) finished and, most importantly, well documented. In fact, the code itself is published as examples of (semi-)literate programming. Every new project and change is automatically published and open for discussion.

The website works as a 'static site generator' in which the code files are the flatpages. A special comment-tag indicates what comments should be parsed as YAML metadata and HTML (from Markdown). This limits the projects to single files, but I think that would cover most of it.

About me

I am Roy Prins, 35 years old, and I have no formal background in programming. I run a one-person consultancy firm: Snirp and my mind is always full of stuff to improve and build. I see no reason that is ever going to change and I think that being a good programmer would go a long way to help me build the stuff.
